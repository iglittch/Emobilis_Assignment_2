About

Kenya, country in East Africa famed for its scenic landscapes and vast wildlife preserves. Its Indian Ocean coast provided historically important ports by which goods from Arabian and Asian traders have entered the continent for many centuries.

Along that coast, which holds some of the finest beaches in Africa, are predominantly Muslim Swahili cities such as Mombasa, a historic centre that has contributed much to the musical and culinary heritage of the country. Inland are populous highlands famed for both their tea plantations, an economic staple during the British colonial era, and their variety of animal species, including lions, elephants, cheetahs, rhinoceroses, and hippopotamuses.

Kenya’s western provinces, marked by lakes and rivers, are forested, while a small portion of the north is desert and semidesert. The country’s diverse wildlife and panoramic geography draw large numbers of European and North American visitors, and tourism is an important contributor to Kenya’s economy

Mount Kenya is the source of the name of the Republic of Kenya. German explorer Johann Ludwig Krapf penned the first known version of the current name in the 1800s. Krapf saw the mountain peak and inquired as to its name while traveling with a Kamba caravan headed by the long-distance trader Chief Kivoi. The reason Kivoi told him "Kĩ-Nyaa" or "Kĩlĩma- Kĩinyaa" is likely that the pattern of black granite with white snow on its peaks reminded him of an ostrich's feathers.An exceptionally dazzling object is referred to in archaic Kikuyu as "nyaga," or more frequently, "manyaganyaga." Mount Kenya is known by the Agikuyu, who live on its slopes, as KĪrĩma Kĩrĩnyaga (roughly, "the mountain with brightness" in Kikuyu), while the Embu refer to it as "Kirinyaa."

The name was written by Ludwig Krapf as both Kenia and Kegnia. According to some, this was an accurate transcription of the African pronunciation, /ˈkɛnjə/.The Scottish geologist and naturalist Joseph Thompsons labeled Mt. Kenya as Mt. Kenia on a map he drew in 1882.Pars pro toto, the name of the peak was recognized as the national name. When the nation was known as the East African Protectorate in the early colonial era, it was not widely used by the government. The Colony of Kenya became the new official name in 1920.
